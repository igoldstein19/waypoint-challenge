The Problem

Almost 10 million students in the U.S. have Individualized Education Programs (IEPs), legally binding 20+ page documents that describe exactly what a student needs in the classroom every day. But most teachers can't act on them well. Not because they don't care, but the support is lacking.

To differentiate a lesson for a student with an IEP, you need to understand the curriculum deeply, understand the student's specific goals and accommodations, and then figure out how to adapt a specific lesson. It's a complex task that special education teachers need to spend hours on when they only have minutes.

This is where AI can make a real difference: not generating generic worksheets but actually reasoning about the intersection of a curriculum, a lesson, and a student's specific needs.

The Challenge

Build an MCP server that connects to Claude and helps a teacher differentiate instruction. We'll provide sample lessons and a sample (anonymized) IEP. Your server should take the lesson and provide the teachers with specific strategies and resources to support the student in the classroom. The product should give a teacher what they need to support that student with a click of a button rather than hours of prep.

What you'll build

  • An MCP server (TypeScript or Python) that exposes curriculum docs and IEP data as resources/tools to Claude
  • The server should enable Claude to reason about the specific lesson content and the specific student needs, and produce concrete, actionable modifications
  • Think: scaffolded questions, modified materials, alternative assessments, accommodation reminders — things a teacher can actually use. Feel free to research about Universal Design of Learning (UDL) practices and other pedagogical principles.

What we'll provide

  • Sample lessons (a few lessons from a real K-8 curriculum)
  • Sample IEP document (anonymized)
A note on scope

This is intentionally open-ended. There's no single "right" architecture. We want to see how you think about structuring domain data for an LLM: what becomes a resource, what becomes a tool, how you handle context, and whether the output is actually useful to a teacher. A thoughtful, well-structured solution that handles one lesson well is better than a sprawling system that handles many lessons poorly.

Evaluation Criteria

Output quality
Are the instructional modifications specific, actionable, and grounded in both the curriculum and the IEP?
Architecture decisions
How did you structure curriculum and IEP data for Claude? What trade-offs did you make and why?
Code quality
Clean, readable, well-organized. Comments where they matter, and tests if you have time.
Domain understanding
Does the solution show you thought about what a teacher actually needs, not just what's technically interesting?
